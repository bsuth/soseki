<h3 class="section">Rough translation</h3>
<p>The professor laughed out loud. "I'm no such romantic. Compared to you, I'm outright prosaic."</p>
<p>"Even so, if that girl had reappeared, I'll bet you would have married her."</p>
<p>"I wonder ..." He paused for a moment to reflect. "I suppose I might have."</p>
<p>Sanshirō looked at him sympathetically, compelling him to continue. "To attribute my bachelorhood to that girl is to imply that she somehow handicapped me. Some folks, however, are inherently unfit for marriage. And others, due to circumstance, are rendered unmarriageable."</p>
<p>"I can't imagine there being many such circumstances."</p>
<p>The professor looked through his smoke toward Sanshirō. "Hamlet, you'll recall, had no desire to marry. There may only be one Hamlet, but there are others like him."</p>
<p>"What sort of others?"</p>
<p>"For example," the professor said, then fell silent. Smoke surged forth. "For example, imagine a man who lost his father early. Imagine his mother has raised him up alone. The mother falls ill, and finally, as she's breathing her last, she tells him to seek out a certain so-and-so after she's gone. He's never met this certain person, never even heard of him. When he asks her why, she doesn't answer. He persists, and she reveals in a weak voice that so-and-so is his true father. -- It's just a story, but imagine a man with such a mother. It's only natural, isn't it, that his faith in marriage be shattered."</p>
<p>"There can't be many such cases."</p>
<p>"There aren't so many, but there are some."</p>
<p>"But surely that story's not yours."</p>
<p>The professor laughed quietly. "Your mother's still living, isn't she?"</p>
<p>"Yes."</p>
<p>"And your father?"</p>
<p>"He's passed away."</p>
<p>"The year after the Constitution was promulgated, my mother died."</p>
