<h3 class="section">Rough translation</h3>
<p>"I'd rather it were something serious. These minor colds are the worst thing of all." Sensei looked at me with a wry smile as he spoke.</p>
<p>This from a man who'd never in his life been seriously ill. I couldn't help but smile.</p> 
<p>"I'd gladly take a cold in exchange for something worse. I expect you would too, if you once fell truly ill."</p>
<p>"I don't know. If I ever do fall ill, then I'd just as well fall deathly ill."</p>
<p>I dismissed this as idle chatter, proceeded to relate the contents of my mother's letter, and asked if I might borrow some money.</p>
<p>"I'm sorry to hear about your father. We should have adequate cash on hand."</p>
<p>Sensei summoned his wife and had her bring the required amount. She carefully laid out the money, which she'd gone in and pulled from a drawer in the cupboard or elsewhere, on a sheet of white paper that she'd set out before me. "You must be concerned."</p>
<p>"Are his fainting spells frequent?" Sensei asked.</p>
<p>"There was nothing about that in the letter -- is it something that recurs?"</p>
<p>"Yes."</p>
<p>I learned for the first time that Sensei's wife's mother had died of the same illness.</p>
<p>"It doesn't sound good," I said.</p>
<p>"I'm afraid not. I'd take his place for you if I could -- has he felt nauseous?"</p>
<p>"I don't know. There was no mention of it, so I expect not."</p>
<p>"If he's not nauseous then he's still all right," Sensei's wife added.</p>
<p>I left Tōkyō that same day on the evening train.</p>
